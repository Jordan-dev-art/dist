Buying a Car From Another State? Use Auto Transport

How to coordinate purchase and transport for an out-of-state vehicle acquisition.

Understanding the Auto Transport Industry

The auto transport industry moves millions of vehicles each year across the United States. Most consumers interact with a freight broker — a licensed intermediary (like OK Car Shipping LLC, MC-987654) that connects shippers with a network of independent motor carriers. Brokers don't own trucks; they use their carrier network and dispatch expertise to find the best match for your shipment.

Understanding this structure helps you set the right expectations. When you book with a broker, you're not locked into a specific truck — you're buying access to a nationwide carrier network with competitive pricing. The broker handles dispatch, insurance verification, and customer support from booking through delivery.

Key Factors That Affect Your Shipment

Whether you're shipping a sedan across the state or a classic car coast-to-coast, these variables will shape your experience and your quote:

1. Distance and Route Popularity

The most obvious factor is distance — but it's not the only one. Route popularity matters enormously. High-traffic lanes like Oklahoma to Florida or California to New York have dozens of carriers running them weekly, which keeps prices competitive. Less-traveled routes may cost more per mile simply because carrier availability is lower.

2. Vehicle Type and Condition

Larger vehicles (SUVs, trucks, vans) take up more space on a carrier and weigh more, which costs more to transport. Non-running vehicles require special loading equipment (winch or forklift) and typically add $150–$250 to the quote. Low-clearance vehicles may require enclosed transport or specific ramps.

3. Transport Type: Open vs. Enclosed

Open transport is the industry standard and accounts for 90%+ of all auto shipments. Your vehicle rides exposed on a multi-car trailer alongside 7–9 other vehicles. It's safe, reliable, and the most affordable option.

Enclosed transport places your vehicle inside a covered, sealed trailer. It's 40–60% more expensive but provides maximum protection. If your vehicle is worth $50,000+, is a collector/classic car, or has been freshly detailed or painted, enclosed is worth the premium.

4. Season and Timing

Auto transport pricing is supply-and-demand driven. Two peak seasons drive up prices:

  • Snowbird Season (Oct–Dec, Feb–Apr) — Massive northbound/southbound demand to/from Florida, Arizona, and other warm-weather states
  • Summer Moving Season (June–August) — Military PCS moves, college students, and general residential relocations spike nationwide

Booking 2–4 weeks in advance during peak seasons is strongly recommended.

5. Pickup Flexibility

The more flexible your pickup window, the better your pricing. A rigid "must pickup on this specific date" requirement limits which carriers can take your load, reducing competition and driving up the rate. A 3–5 day flexible window typically saves $50–$150.

How to Get the Best Quote for Your Car Shipment

  1. Use a reputable broker with an active FMCSA MC number. Verify at safer.fmcsa.dot.gov.
  2. Compare 2–3 quotes but be wary of prices that seem too low — they often signal bait-and-switch tactics (quote low, increase price at pickup).
  3. Read the fine print — understand when payment is due, what the cancellation policy is, and what cargo insurance covers.
  4. Check reviews on Transport Reviews, Google, and the BBB. Look for consistent patterns, not just star ratings.
  5. Avoid brokers who require upfront deposits before assigning a carrier. Legitimate brokers collect payment at or after delivery.

Preparing Your Vehicle for Transport

A little preparation goes a long way toward a smooth shipping experience:

  • 📷 Photograph everything — Document all existing scratches, dents, chips, and interior condition with timestamps
  • ⛽ Leave ¼ tank of fuel — Enough to drive on/off the carrier, but not so much it adds unnecessary weight
  • 🧹 Remove personal items — Cargo insurance only covers the vehicle, not belongings left inside
  • 🔇 Disable the alarm — Or leave the deactivation code with the carrier to avoid issues mid-transport
  • 🔑 Provide a spare key if possible — Some carriers require key access for loading/unloading
  • 🔧 Fix fluid leaks — Leaking vehicles can damage other cars on the carrier and may be refused

What to Expect at Delivery

When the carrier arrives at your destination, conduct a thorough inspection before signing the final Bill of Lading. Walk around the entire vehicle and compare its condition to your pre-pickup photos. Check for:

  • New scratches, dents, or dings
  • Cracked or chipped glass
  • Paint transfer or scuff marks
  • Any damage to the undercarriage

If you find damage, note it on the Bill of Lading before signing and contact the broker immediately. Do not sign a clean Bill of Lading if there is visible damage — this can complicate or void your insurance claim.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does car shipping cost?

Average car shipping costs range from $350 for short-haul routes to $1,400+ for cross-country shipments. The biggest factors are distance, vehicle size, transport type (open vs. enclosed), and seasonal demand.

Is car shipping safe?

Yes. The vast majority of vehicles are shipped without incident. All FMCSA-licensed carriers maintain active cargo insurance. Proper vehicle preparation and thorough inspection at pickup and delivery further reduce risk.

How long does car shipping take?

Transit time ranges from 1–2 days for regional moves (under 500 miles) to 7–10 days for cross-country routes. Add 1–3 days for carrier dispatch after booking.

Should I ship my car or drive it?

For moves over 1,000 miles, car shipping is often more cost-effective when you factor in fuel, hotels, meals, and wear on the vehicle. It also protects your car's resale value by keeping mileage off the odometer.
